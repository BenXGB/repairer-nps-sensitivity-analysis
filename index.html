Customer Analytics Case Study

Author: Ben Ingram

Business Context:

A motor insurance business has a series of partner repair shops across the country which repair customers’ vehicle in the event of a claim. There is a likelihood to recommend survey which gets sent to some customers after their vehicle repair has been completed. The survey result gives insight to how well each repair shop handles various aspects of the customer interation (communication, timeliness etc).

Problem Statement:

The business wants to improve their overall net promoter score by +10 points over the next six months. There are over 400 repair shops - some shops only repair a handful of vehicles each week and others repair 100+. The Executive Manager wants to improve the overall NPS by targeting low performing repairers but also does not want to waste training resources on low volume shops.

Which shops should we direct training resources to for the biggest uplift in NPS?

Approach

Ranking repairers purely by raw NPS can overemphasise low-volume outliers and lead to poor resource allocation decisions. In practice, a moderately underperforming repairer with high survey volume may have a far greater impact on overall portfolio NPS than a severely underperforming repairer handling only a small number of claims.

Lets assess the impact of each repairer on overall NPS using a leave one out sensitivity approach. For each repairer, we recalculate NPS excluding all surveys associated with them and compare this to the baseline NPS.

This produces a marginal impact score:

\(Impact(X) = NPS(without X) − NPS(overall)\)

For example, excluding AutoFix Ltd surveys increases overall NPS by +1.5 points, indicating it is a net drag on our average customer experience.

This allows us to rank repairers by their relative contribution to overall satisfaction and focus training/operational resources where we will see the biggest improvement.

Example Base Query - Let’s just calculate NPS per month to establish the general foundation for the rest of the analysis:

SELECT DATE_TRUNC('month', completed_date) AS survey_month
        , 100 * (SUM(CASE WHEN score_category = 'promoter' THEN 1 ELSE 0 END) 
        - SUM(CASE WHEN score_category = 'detractor' THEN 1 ELSE 0 END))::float
        / COUNT(*) AS nps_score -- we would wrap this in a nullif or similar in a production query to avoid divide by zero errors
        , COUNT(*) AS total_surveys
FROM surveys
WHERE completed_date >= DATE_TRUNC('month', CURRENT_DATE) - INTERVAL '3 months' -- we'll take a 3 month window to smooth out volatility
        AND completed_date < DATE_TRUNC('month', CURRENT_DATE) -- dont include developing month
GROUP BY survey_month
ORDER BY survey_month;

CTEs - A few handy blocks we will reference later

Our baseline NPS over a three month window excluding the developing month

WITH cte_overall_nps AS (
    SELECT 100 * (SUM(CASE WHEN score_category = 'promoter' THEN 1 ELSE 0 END) 
        - SUM(CASE WHEN score_category = 'detractor' THEN 1 ELSE 0 END))
        / COUNT(*) AS overall_nps
    FROM surveys
    WHERE completed_date >= DATE_TRUNC('month', CURRENT_DATE) - INTERVAL '3 months'
        AND completed_date < DATE_TRUNC('month', CURRENT_DATE)
)

A unique list of repairers in our time frame - will make things easier when we calculate NPS ex. repairers

, cte_repairer_list AS (
    SELECT DISTINCT repairer
    FROM surveys
    WHERE completed_date >= DATE_TRUNC('month', CURRENT_DATE) - INTERVAL '3 months'
        AND completed_date < DATE_TRUNC('month', CURRENT_DATE)
)

NPS calculation excluding each repairer from our list

, cte_leave_one_out AS (
    SELECT r.repairer
    , COUNT(*) AS remaining_surveys
    , 100 * (SUM(CASE WHEN score_category = 'promoter' THEN 1 ELSE 0 END) 
        - SUM(CASE WHEN score_category = 'detractor' THEN 1 ELSE 0 END))
        / COUNT(*) AS nps_without_repairer
    FROM cte_repairer_list r
    INNER JOIN surveys s
        ON r.repairer <> s.repairer -- we want to exclude the repairers own surveys to see the impact of their removal on overall NPS
    WHERE s.completed_date >= DATE_TRUNC('month', CURRENT_DATE) - INTERVAL '3 months'
        AND s.completed_date < DATE_TRUNC('month', CURRENT_DATE)
    GROUP BY r.repairer
)

Per repairer NPS and survey count for full information in our final output. We could combine this with the above CTE but will keep separate for code readability.

, cte_repairer_metrics AS (
    SELECT r.repairer
    , COUNT(*) AS total_surveys
    , 100 * (SUM(CASE WHEN score_category = 'promoter' THEN 1 ELSE 0 END) 
        - SUM(CASE WHEN score_category = 'detractor' THEN 1 ELSE 0 END))
        / COUNT(*) AS repairer_nps
    FROM cte_repairer_list r
    INNER JOIN surveys s
        ON r.repairer = s.repairer
    WHERE s.completed_date >= DATE_TRUNC('month', CURRENT_DATE) - INTERVAL '3 months'
        AND s.completed_date < DATE_TRUNC('month', CURRENT_DATE)
    GROUP BY r.repairer
)

Final query - Bring together the CTEs, calculate the impact, and rank accordingly

SELECT l.repairer
, o.overall_nps
, l.nps_without_repairer
, m.repairer_nps
, m.total_surveys
, ROUND(l.nps_without_repairer - o.overall_nps, 2) AS nps_impact
, CASE WHEN l.nps_without_repairer > o.overall_nps THEN 'Drag on NPS'
       WHEN l.nps_without_repairer < o.overall_nps THEN 'Positive Impact'
       ELSE 'Neutral' END AS impact_category
FROM cte_leave_one_out l
INNER JOIN cte_repairer_metrics m
    ON l.repairer = m.repairer
CROSS JOIN cte_overall_nps o
ORDER BY nps_impact DESC
LIMIT 5;

Recommendation and further thoughts

The business should allocate training resources to the repair shops listed for the largest impact to overall NPS. Note we will naturally hit diminishing returns as we work down the list and review each month.

Ideas for further analysis:

  • Set up a tracking framework to ensure repair shops are improving post corrective action / training implementation
  • Build an additional metric tracking how frequently each repairer appears in the top 5 over the past 12 months. This helps distinguish persistent operational issues from short-term volatility driven by temporary factors such as management changes, staffing disruption etc.
  • Order by ascending impact score to see if theres anything we can learn from our best performing repair shops

Considerations

  • Results should be interpreted alongside operational context such as claim complexity and region
  • A repairer with low NPS impact may still warrant intervention if complaint severity is high
